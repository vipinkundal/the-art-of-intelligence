26. Logical And Problem-Solving Patterns To Recognize

These reasoning, algorithmic, foundation-model, and scientific patterns recur across nearly every branch of AI. Recognizing them helps you understand papers, design systems, debug failures, and transfer ideas between fields.

Pattern recognition workflow

Name the pattern Identify whether a problem needs reasoning, search, optimization, evidence, decomposition, or control.
Match the mechanism Choose a method such as dynamic programming, sampling, retrieval, verification, or ablation.
Test the result Check assumptions, compare baselines, inspect failures, and look for counterexamples.

Reasoning patterns

  • Deduction: Derives conclusions that must follow from stated premises.
  • Induction: Generalizes from observed examples to broader patterns.
  • Abduction: Chooses the best explanation for incomplete or surprising evidence.
  • Analogy: Transfers structure from a familiar problem to a new one.
  • Bayesian updating: Revises beliefs as evidence changes likelihoods.
  • Causal intervention: Asks what would happen if a variable were actively changed.
  • Counterfactual analysis: Studies what would have happened under a different condition.
  • Case-based reasoning: Solves new problems by adapting prior similar cases.
  • Means-ends analysis: Compares current state to goal state and chooses actions that reduce the gap.
  • Hypothesis generation and testing: Proposes explanations and designs tests that can support or reject them.

Algorithmic patterns

  • Divide and conquer: Splits a problem into smaller independent subproblems and combines their answers.
  • Dynamic programming: Reuses solutions to overlapping subproblems.
  • Memoization: Caches repeated computations so they are not recomputed.
  • Greedy optimization: Makes locally best choices when that strategy is justified or useful as a heuristic.
  • Branch and bound: Searches candidate solutions while pruning regions that cannot beat the best known answer.
  • Backtracking: Builds partial solutions and retreats when constraints are violated.
  • Constraint propagation: Uses known constraints to shrink the set of possible values.
  • Relaxation: Replaces a hard problem with an easier approximate version.
  • Alternating optimization: Optimizes one set of variables at a time while holding others fixed.
  • Expectation-maximization: Alternates between estimating hidden variables and updating model parameters.
  • Sampling: Uses random draws to approximate distributions, expectations, or search spaces.
  • Importance weighting: Reweights examples or samples to correct for distribution mismatch.
  • Ensemble averaging: Combines multiple predictors to reduce variance or improve robustness.
  • Bagging: Trains models on resampled data and averages predictions.
  • Boosting: Trains later models to focus on examples earlier models handled poorly.
  • Self-play: Generates training experience through agents competing or interacting with versions of themselves.
  • Curriculum learning: Orders tasks from easier to harder to shape learning.

Modern foundation-model patterns

  • Retrieve then generate: Finds external context before producing an answer.
  • Decompose then solve: Breaks a task into smaller subproblems before answering.
  • Generate then verify: Produces candidate answers and checks them with tests, critics, tools, or constraints.
  • Generate then rank: Samples candidates and selects the best one by score or preference.
  • Propose then critique: Creates a plan or answer, then reviews it for flaws.
  • Plan then execute: Builds an explicit sequence of actions before using tools or taking steps.
  • Route then specialize: Sends inputs to specialized models, tools, experts, prompts, or workflows.
  • Sample then vote: Aggregates multiple sampled reasoning paths or answers.
  • Search over reasoning traces: Explores candidate chains of thought, plans, or proof paths before choosing one.
  • Use tools for exact operations: Delegates computation, lookup, code execution, retrieval, or structured actions to tools.
  • Ground claims in external evidence: Connects answers to retrieved documents, databases, citations, logs, or observations.
  • Maintain explicit state: Stores task state, intermediate results, memory, or plans outside the model context alone.
  • Escalate uncertain cases to humans: Routes ambiguous, high-risk, or low-confidence cases to human review.

Scientific patterns

  • Establish a simple baseline: Start with the simplest credible comparison before adding complexity.
  • Change one variable at a time: Isolate causes by controlling all other factors.
  • Run ablations: Remove or vary components to test whether they actually matter.
  • Separate training and evaluation data: Prevent evaluation from leaking into training or tuning.
  • Measure variance across runs: Check whether results survive random seeds, sampling, initialization, and data order.
  • Inspect failures, not only averages: Look at examples, slices, and edge cases behind aggregate metrics.
  • Test out-of-distribution behaviour: Evaluate whether behavior holds beyond the original data distribution.
  • Attempt to falsify your own hypothesis: Design tests that could prove your preferred explanation wrong.
  • Distinguish correlation from causation: Avoid treating predictive association as evidence of causal effect.

Why it matters for AI

AI techniques often look unrelated until you recognize the shared pattern underneath. A retrieval system, an agent workflow, an RL algorithm, a causal study, and a scientific paper can all use the same deeper moves: decompose, search, update, verify, compare, intervene, and measure.

Learning target

You should be able to recognize recurring reasoning, algorithmic, foundation-model, and scientific patterns, explain when each pattern is useful, and use them to read AI methods, design experiments, diagnose failures, and choose practical solution strategies.
