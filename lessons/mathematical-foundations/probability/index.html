1. Mathematical Foundations

1.3 Probability

Probability is the language of uncertainty. It helps AI systems reason from incomplete data, model noise, estimate confidence, sample possibilities, and compare competing explanations.

The simple idea

Probability assigns numbers to uncertain events. In AI, those numbers help models express how likely an output is, how much evidence supports a prediction, and how uncertain a decision remains.

Events Define what can happen and assign probabilities to each possible outcome.
Evidence Condition on observations to update beliefs and compare explanations.
Decisions Use expectations, uncertainty, and sampling to choose or evaluate actions.

Understand

  • Sample spaces and events: The sample space lists possible outcomes; an event is a subset of outcomes you care about.
  • Conditional probability: Updates the chance of an event after you know another event has occurred.
  • Independence and conditional independence: Independence means one event gives no information about another; conditional independence means that after controlling for a third variable.
  • Random variables: Functions that assign numeric or categorical values to uncertain outcomes.
  • Probability mass and density functions: PMFs handle discrete outcomes; densities describe continuous outcomes through areas under a curve.
  • Cumulative distributions: Give the probability that a random variable is less than or equal to a value.
  • Joint, marginal, and conditional distributions: Joint distributions model variables together, marginals focus on one variable, and conditionals update one variable given another.
  • Expectation: The long-run average value of a random variable under its distribution.
  • Variance and covariance: Variance measures spread; covariance measures how two variables move together.
  • Correlation: A normalized covariance that ranges from negative to positive association.
  • Law of total probability: Breaks a probability into cases and adds their weighted contributions.
  • Bayes' theorem: Reverses conditioning so evidence can update beliefs about hidden causes.
  • Law of large numbers: Sample averages converge toward expected values as sample size grows.
  • Central limit theorem: Many averaged random effects become approximately Gaussian under broad conditions.
  • Markov chains: Model sequences where the next state depends on the current state, not the full history.
  • Markov processes: Generalize memoryless state transitions over time.
  • Monte Carlo estimation: Uses random samples to approximate expectations or probabilities that are hard to compute exactly.
  • Importance sampling: Samples from a more convenient distribution and reweights samples to estimate the target distribution.
  • Rejection sampling: Samples from a proposal distribution and accepts only samples that fit the target shape.

Essential formulas

Bayes' theorem P(A | B) = P(B | A)P(A) / P(B)
Expectation, discrete E[X] = sum_x x P(X = x)
Expectation, continuous E[X] = integral x p(x) dx
Variance Var(X) = E[(X - E[X])^2]
Covariance Cov(X, Y) = E[(X - E[X])(Y - E[Y])]

Important distributions

  • Bernoulli: Models one yes-or-no trial, such as click or no click.
  • Binomial: Counts successes across a fixed number of Bernoulli trials.
  • Categorical: Models one choice among several classes.
  • Multinomial: Counts how many times each class appears across repeated categorical trials.
  • Uniform: Gives equal probability to each value in a range or set.
  • Gaussian: Models bell-shaped continuous variation around a mean.
  • Multivariate Gaussian: Extends Gaussian structure to vectors with covariance between dimensions.
  • Poisson: Counts events occurring in a fixed interval when events are rare and independent.
  • Exponential: Models waiting time between events in a Poisson process.
  • Beta: Models uncertainty over a probability between zero and one.
  • Gamma: Models positive continuous quantities such as waiting times or rates.
  • Dirichlet: Models uncertainty over a vector of probabilities that sum to one.
  • Student's t: Handles heavier-tailed uncertainty, often when sample sizes are small.
  • Log-normal: Models positive values whose logarithm is Gaussian.
  • Gumbel: Models extremes and appears in differentiable sampling tricks.

Why it matters for AI

Many AI methods are probabilistic: classifiers estimate class probabilities, language models predict token distributions, Bayesian methods update beliefs, and sampling methods approximate quantities that are hard to compute exactly.

Probability also supports evaluation and reliability by helping you reason about uncertainty, variance, confidence, rare events, and expected outcomes.

Learning target

After this lesson, you should be able to explain conditional probability, Bayes' theorem, expectation, variance, covariance, common distributions, and why sampling is useful in machine learning.
