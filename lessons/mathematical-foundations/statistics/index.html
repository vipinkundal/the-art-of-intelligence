1.4 Statistics

Statistics helps turn data into evidence. It gives AI builders tools for estimating parameters, measuring uncertainty, testing claims, designing experiments, and detecting when data changes.

The simple idea

Statistics asks what we can learn from limited data and how confident we should be. In AI, that matters whenever we train, compare, evaluate, deploy, or monitor a model.

Essential formulas

Maximum likelihood theta_hat_MLE = arg max_theta sum_i log p_theta(x_i)
MAP estimation theta_hat_MAP = arg max_theta [sum_i log p_theta(x_i) + log p(theta)]
Bias-variance decomposition E[(y - f_hat(x))^2] = Bias^2 + Variance + Irreducible noise

Why it matters for AI

Statistics helps you decide whether a model improvement is real, whether a dataset represents the target population, and whether predictions remain calibrated after deployment.

Estimation methods such as MLE and MAP connect data, likelihoods, priors, and model parameters. Bias-variance reasoning explains why models can underfit, overfit, or fail under distribution shift.

Learning target

After this lesson, you should be able to explain samples versus populations, MLE, MAP, confidence intervals, p-values, bias and variance, calibration, and distribution shift in model evaluation.
