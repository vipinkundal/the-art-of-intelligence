17. Multimodal AI

Native multimodality is now a core frontier topic, covering combinations of text, image, video, audio, action, and sensor inputs.

Multimodal pipeline

Encode Turn text, images, audio, video, or actions into model-readable representations.
Align and fuse Connect representations so words, regions, sounds, and events can refer to each other.
Ground output Answer, retrieve, generate, or act while staying tied to visible or audible evidence.

Learn

  • Contrastive vision-language learning: Trains image and text representations so matching pairs become close and mismatched pairs separate.
  • Image-text alignment: Connects visual content with words, captions, labels, or instructions.
  • Vision encoders: Convert images or frames into feature vectors or tokens for downstream reasoning.
  • Projection layers: Map outputs from one modality encoder into the shared space expected by another model.
  • Cross-attention fusion: Lets tokens from one modality attend to tokens from another modality.
  • Early, intermediate and late fusion: Fuse modalities near the input, inside the model, or near the final decision.
  • Multimodal tokenization: Represents non-text inputs as discrete tokens, patches, frames, or embeddings.
  • Image and video instruction tuning: Teaches models to follow natural-language instructions grounded in visual inputs.
  • Visual question answering: Answers questions that require reading or reasoning over an image.
  • Image captioning: Generates text that describes the visible content of an image.
  • Document understanding: Combines OCR, layout, tables, figures, and text to interpret documents.
  • Speech-language models: Connect spoken audio with transcription, dialogue, translation, or reasoning tasks.
  • Audio-language models: Interpret sounds, music, events, or speech in relation to text.
  • Text-to-image generation: Produces images conditioned on natural-language prompts or structured controls.
  • Text-to-video generation: Generates sequences of frames while maintaining temporal consistency.
  • Multimodal retrieval: Searches across modalities, such as finding images from text or text from images.
  • Vision-language-action models: Connect perception and instructions to actions in robotics or UI control.
  • Multimodal agents: Use models, tools, memory, and perception to act across visual, textual, audio, or physical environments.

Important design questions

  • How are modalities tokenized? Tokenization determines what the model can see, compress, align, and generate.
  • Where are they fused? Fusion location affects compute cost, reasoning depth, and how strongly modalities influence each other.
  • Are encoders frozen or trained jointly? Frozen encoders are cheaper and stable; joint training can improve alignment but costs more.
  • How is temporal information represented? Video and audio need ordering, duration, motion, and timing cues.
  • How are grounding and hallucination evaluated? Evaluation must check whether outputs are supported by the actual image, video, audio, or document.
  • Does the model connect words to identifiable regions, sounds, or actions? Strong multimodal systems should tie language to evidence, not just plausible text.

Learning target

You should be able to compare alignment, fusion, tokenization, and grounding strategies across image-text, audio-language, video-language, and vision-language-action systems.
