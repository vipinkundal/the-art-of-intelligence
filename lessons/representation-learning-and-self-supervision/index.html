11. Representation Learning And Self-Supervision

Representation learning studies how models turn raw inputs into useful embeddings, while self-supervision creates training signals from the data itself.

Representation loop

Encode Turn raw text, images, audio, or events into embeddings.
Compare Use similarity, contrast, prediction, or masking to shape the embedding space.
Transfer Reuse the learned representation for search, classification, generation, or control.

Study

  • Distributed word representations: Represent words as dense vectors whose geometry captures usage and meaning.
  • Word2vec: Learns word embeddings from local context prediction tasks.
  • Skip-gram: Predicts surrounding words from a center word.
  • Continuous bag of words: Predicts a center word from surrounding context words.
  • Negative sampling: Makes contrastive word training efficient by comparing positives against sampled negatives.
  • GloVe: Learns embeddings from global word co-occurrence statistics.
  • FastText: Builds word vectors from subword units, helping with rare or unseen words.
  • Metric learning: Trains embeddings so distances match semantic similarity or task relevance.
  • Siamese networks: Use shared encoders to compare two inputs in the same embedding space.
  • Triplet loss: Pulls an anchor closer to a positive example than to a negative example by a margin.
  • Contrastive learning: Learns by bringing related views together and pushing unrelated examples apart.
  • InfoNCE: A contrastive loss that scores one positive against many negatives.
  • SimCLR: Learns visual representations by contrasting augmented views of the same image.
  • MoCo: Maintains a moving queue of negative examples for contrastive learning.
  • CLIP: Aligns image and text embeddings using contrastive training on paired captions and images.
  • BYOL: Learns representations by predicting another network's view without explicit negatives.
  • DINO: Uses self-distillation to learn strong visual features without labels.
  • Masked autoencoders: Reconstruct missing parts of inputs from visible context.
  • Masked language modelling: Predicts hidden tokens from surrounding text.
  • Predictive coding: Learns by predicting future, missing, or compressed signals.
  • Joint-embedding predictive architectures: Predict representations of target observations rather than reconstructing raw pixels or tokens.

InfoNCE

L = -log(exp(sim(q, k+) / tau) / sum_j exp(sim(q, k_j) / tau))

Triplet loss

L = max(0, d(a, p) - d(a, n) + m)

Learning target

You should be able to explain why good embeddings preserve task-relevant similarity, how contrastive objectives separate positives from negatives, and how masked or predictive tasks create labels without manual annotation.
